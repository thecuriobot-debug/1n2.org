The UK government on Wednesday released files on internal discussions leading to its appointment of Labour Party veteran Peter Mandelson as US ambassador in 2024.

The papers said that Prime Minister Keir Starmer was advised that his former communications chief was "satisfied" with Mandelson's answers on his ties to Jeffrey Epstein. They also detailed how the Labour grandee had asked for a settlement payment of more than half a million pounds when he was later dismissed from the role.

Chief Secretary to the Prime Minister Darren Jones told the House of Commons on Wednesday that some details were not being released because of their relevance to an ongoing police investigation.

"Whilst the documents point to public reports of an ongoing relationship between Peter Mandelson and Jeffrey Epstein, the advice did not expose the depth and extent of their relationship," Jones told parliament.

What is the Mandelson scandal about?

Mandelson was already a known associate of deceased US financier and sex offender Jeffrey Epstein when he was given the high-profile diplomat's posting in Washington.

Further allegations and email releases led to his dismissal last September.

Since the last US Justice Department release of information, Mandelson has had to quit the Labour Party and the House of Lords, and faces a police investigation on suspsicion of misconduct in public office, not unlike former Prince Andrew.

The case has put pressure on Prime Minister Starmer's government, given its decision to give Mandelson a prime job despite his known links to Epstein and an already checkered past involving two past high-profile resignations from Tony Blair's governments.

Starmer had earlier said that Mandelson told "lies" when describing the extent of his relationship with Epstein in 2024 as he was under consideration for the ambassador's role, which a document written amid his dismissal last September appeared to reinforce.

"The emails revealed a depth and extent of a relationship with Epstein which he [Starmer] had not been aware of previously when he made the decision to appoint Mandelson," the letter read. "On this basis, he proposed to ask Mandelson to resign."

What did the internal papers say about the vetting and appointment process?

One document released, dated December 11, 2024, was marked "official sensitive" and labeled "Advice to the Prime Minister." It said how Starmer had expressed a preference for a political appointment to the US ambassador's role, with the return of Donald Trump to the White House looming.

It said he had discussed candidates with his then-chief of staff, Morgan McSweeney, with the "lead candidate being Peter Mandelson."

McSweeney "has discussed Peter's relationship with Jeffrey Epstein which we will go through with you," the note says, adding that the then director of communications "is satisfied with his responses about contact."

Starmer was also warned of a "general reputational risk" in appointing Epstein, noting how JP Morgan bank had commissioned a 2019 report that found Mandelson had maintained a "a particularly close relationship" with Epstein even after his first conviction for child sex offenses.

Starmer had previously said Mandelson had portrayed Epstein "as someone he barely knew" and that he had fired him on learning this to be a lie.

When he was fired last year, Mandelson sought a settlement fee of almost 550,000 pounds (roughly €635,000 or $735,000 at today's exchange rates) from the British government, albeit ultimately settling for 75,000 pounds, another document showed.

"It's astonishing Mandelson received a payout even after his longstanding connections to Epstein were discovered," Liberal Democrat leader Ed Davey said in the Commons on Wednesday, calling for the money to be donated to charity. "These files show beyond doubt the prime minister was made well aware of the risks of his appointment. What an insult to the women and girls who suffered at Epstein's hands."

Another email confirms how in 2002, when in a different senior government position and prior to Epstein's first conviction, Mandelson had been instrumental in arranging a meeting between the financier and then Prime Minister Tony Blair.

A letter to Blair, preparing him for the meeting, described Epstein as "very rich," a "friend" of Mandelson, and as "very close to the Duke of York," meaning former Prince Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor.

Who is Peter Mandelson?

Mandelson, 72, has been a fairly prominent figure in British politics since the early 1990s, when he first took a seat in the House of Commons.

He was particularly known for his skills as a strategist and fixer, working as the Labour Party's director of communications even before he took elected office.

Mandelson served in various ministerial roles in the governments of Labour Prime Ministers Tony Blair and Gordon Brown, including Business Secretary and the organizational role of First Secretary of State that is often compared to the role of deputy prime minister, with neither of those positions permanently filled.

He had been no stranger to scandal or to resignation in the past, resigning from Tony Blair's Cabinet in 1998 over an undisclosed loan for a home from a party ally with links to a media mogul. In 2001, he had to resign once more, this time over allegations of seeking to improperly influence a passport application.

Mandelson also had a stint in Brussels as European Commissioner for Trade from 2004 to 2008, in between roles in British governments.

Despite his connection with Epstein, Mandelson himself is not accused of any sexual misconduct.

The decision to give him the job in Washington as Trump returned to the White House was due in part to his reputation as a networker and his ties to wealthy individuals in the US — after one of Britain's most senior traditional civil service diplomats, Kim Darroch, had become a high profile casualty of Trump's first term in 2019.
