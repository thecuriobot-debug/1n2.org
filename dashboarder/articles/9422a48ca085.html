The Kharg move

Al Jazeera reported that military analysts believe the US may suspect Iran is using the island as a staging ground for attacks on ships in the strait. “The Americans are probably targeting it because they think that’s where the Iranians are launching attacks on ships they don’t want to cross the strait,” one analyst told the network.

This dual role explains the US strategy: weaken Iran’s military footprint on Kharg without yet crippling its oil infrastructure.

Trump’s messaging suggests the strike was designed as a warning rather than an attempt to destroy Iran’s energy exports outright. In his statement announcing the attack, the US president said he had refrained from hitting the oil facilities “for reasons of decency” but warned that they could be targeted if Iran interfered with “the Free and Safe Passage of Ships through the Strait of Hormuz.”

Al Jazeera analysis interprets the move as a deliberate signal. By attacking military facilities on Kharg while sparing oil infrastructure, Washington is showing it can degrade Iran’s ability to attack shipping while keeping its most devastating economic option in reserve. The oil infrastructure itself is the real leverage. If destroyed, Iran would lose the primary terminal through which it exports crude, a blow that could remove millions of barrels per day from the global market and severely damage Tehran’s revenue stream.

Iran has warned that such a move would cross a red line. It has threatened to retaliate by striking energy infrastructure across the Gulf region if its own oil facilities are targeted. If the US tries to capture Kharg or destroy its oil infrastructure, it could be almost a do-or-die moment for Iran which can force it to set the Gulf on fire.

Boots on the ground?

At the same time, the US seems to be planning another major provocation, preparing for a possible expansion of the conflict. The Wall Street Journal reported that the Pentagon has approved the deployment of additional Marines and warships to the Middle East as Iranian attacks on shipping intensify in the Strait of Hormuz. US officials told the newspaper that the Japan-based USS Tripoli and its attached Marines are heading toward the region as part of an amphibious ready group.

Such a force typically includes several warships and around 5,000 Marines and sailors. It can be used for rapid military operations ranging from evacuations to amphibious assaults. CNN separately reported that a Marine Expeditionary Unit of roughly 2,500 Marines and sailors is also being deployed, although officials have not publicly confirmed its exact mission.

The nature of an expeditionary unit makes its potential uses clear. It is designed for operations on foreign territory, raising the possibility that the US could eventually attempt to seize strategic ground such as Kharg Island if the conflict escalates further. The deployment signals that Washington is preparing options that go beyond airstrikes.

Late Friday, Trump told reporters that his military campaign will last “as long as necessary.” When asked by reporters how long the war will last, he said: “I can’t tell you that. I mean, I have my own idea.” He added: “I won’t give you a time but we are way ahead of schedule.” The comment may suggest that the US could be planning further military operations which can expand and lengthen the conflict. Deployment of amphibious expeditionary forces does hint at such a possibility. Trump has earlier not ruled out boots on the ground despite risk of heavy casualties.

Iran’s new Hormuz diplomacy

While the US escalates militarily, Iran appears to be pursuing a different strategy of turning control of the Strait of Hormuz into a diplomatic leverage too. Several countries have begun reaching out directly to Tehran to negotiate safe passage for their ships through the strait rather than relying on Washington to guarantee security.

“Key states are now reaching out to the Iranians to try to find a way to negotiate their own safe passage,” Trita Parsi, co-founder of the Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, told Al Jazeera. “India has already struck a deal. The Europeans – France and Italy – have as well. They’ve gone to Iran to do this. Not to Washington.” Parsi said the trend reflects “very clearly who is in control of the situation over there.”

Another report from CNN said Iranian officials are considering allowing certain oil tankers to pass through the strait if the cargo is traded in Chinese yuan rather than US dollars. Such a move would be significant because global oil is overwhelmingly priced and traded in dollars. A yuan-based arrangement would not only bypass US sanctions but could also draw major energy consumers like China and India into separate arrangements with Tehran.

Iran’s moves suggest an attempt to reshape the geopolitical stakes of the conflict. By selectively allowing safe passage for certain countries while restricting others, Iran could divide the international response to the crisis. States heavily dependent on Gulf energy supplies may prioritise keeping trade routes open even if that means negotiating directly with Iran rather than supporting US military pressure. In effect, Hormuz becomes both an economic chokepoint and a diplomatic bargaining tool.

If successful, such a strategy could weaken the cohesion of any US-led coalition against Iran while simultaneously driving up global oil prices. The resulting economic pressure on major importing countries could push them toward accommodation with Iran and de-escalation of tensions in the Gulf .

A dangerous escalation in the making

The latest developments suggest that the Iran war is entering a phase where both sides are raising the stakes in different ways. The US is expanding its military footprint and threatening Iran’s most valuable energy asset. Iran, meanwhile, is experimenting with a strategy that mixes maritime disruption with selective diplomacy toward countries outside the US alliance.

That combination creates a volatile dynamic. A strike on Kharg’s oil facilities could trigger Iranian retaliation against energy infrastructure across the Gulf. At the same time, Iran’s attempt to control access to Hormuz could fracture international consensus and complicate Washington’s strategy.

The result is a conflict that increasingly revolves around the world’s most important energy chokepoint. And as both sides escalate, the Strait of Hormuz is becoming not just the center of the war but the stage on which the global balance of power around oil, shipping and alliances is being tested. Trump's Kharg move, which is seen in response to Iran's Hormuz strategy, has the potential to set the Gulf on fire.
