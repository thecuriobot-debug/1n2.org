The growing paleopathological literature shows that scurvy was not a rare problem among people living in the ancient Asia-Pacific tropics. Scurvy is increasingly identified throughout the region, primarily in children but also in adults. In a study published in the International Journal of Osteoarchaeology, Dr. Chloe Boucher and her colleagues conducted a follow-up paleopathological study of a young adult male from the Philippines' Metal Period (~2000–1800 BP), previously identified as having suffered from hip ankylosis.

The follow-up analysis of the complete skeleton identified signs of scurvy, which likely contributed to the previously identified bone remodeling issues and highlights the importance of considering nutritional deficiencies alongside skeletal evidence of holistic impairment to gain a holistic understanding of how these conditions interact and how individuals lived with and were cared for despite complex, overlapping health challenges in the past.

What is scurvy?

Scurvy results from prolonged vitamin C deficiency, which in turn inhibits the body's collagen production necessary for bone and connective tissue formation, bone remodeling, and reabsorption. Without vitamin C, procollagen cannot mature into stable collagen, leading to various problems, including easy bruising, anemia, swollen gums, osteoporosis, purpuric rashes, and inflammation.

The inflammatory response to a hemorrhage can result in scurvy lesions, which form when clotted blood between the periosteum (membrane of blood vessels around the bone) and the bone forms new connective tissue.

"Scurvy is a micronutrient deficiency, so you don't necessarily need to be starved to get it; you could be relying on foods high in calories but poor in vitamins," explains co-author Dr. Melandri Vlok.

"For example, rice has no detectable Vitamin C, so overreliance can provide you with enough calories to survive, but then you end up short of the necessary Vitamin C to starve off scurvy. Vitamin C is also a water-soluble vitamin, so when you boil food, the vitamin is lost in the water. Even cutting and cooking via other means leads to considerable loss of vitamin C from the original food product."

Yet, despite the abundant and nutritious resources available in tropical Southeast Asia and the Pacific, a number of sites have been identified with individuals suffering from scurvy. Among them is Nagsabaran's Burial 4.

Burial 4 at Nagsabaran

Nagsabaran is a shell midden site located in the northern Philippines on Luzon Island in the Cagayan Valley. It has been dated to the Neolithic with occupation continuing into the Metal Period.

"The Neolithic is the initial occupation period of Nagsabaran and is characterized by smaller farming communities and maritime trade networks limited to Taiwan. These people carried with them domesticated pigs, dogs, and rice. There was only one burial found at the site dating to this period, which did not include any notable grave goods," explained Dr. Vlok.

"The Metal Period, on the other hand, marked more expansive and rigorous maritime trade beyond Taiwan and into China, India, mainland South East Asia, Indonesia, and Oceania. Life in Nagsabaran changed, and their world views likely expanded as far as the seas that connected them to different communities across the Indo-Pacific.

"We see more elaborate and diverse burial practices, like the use of jar burials for some, and they begin to cast and trade in bronze and iron. These vast connections around the world would have also exposed them to new knowledge regarding health and disease, alongside different methods of healing and caring for the sick and injured."

Burial 4 was different from the other 10 burials dated to the Metal Period, explained Dr. Boucher, "No grave goods were found to be associated with burial 4. For the other adults buried at the site, their grave goods typically included Indo-Pacific glass beads, ceramics, metal artifacts, and bits of animal bone (most likely a food offering).

"So, there is perhaps some differential treatment in death for this person. What this difference represents, we don't really know. Interestingly, though, burial 4 was laid to rest only centimeters above the earliest and only domesticated dog burial in the Philippines during this period. We can only speculate a possible connection the community had to domesticated animals, but it does suggest a complex relationship between this community and their environment …"

Permission to analyze Burial 4's remains was originally given in 2009 by the National Museum of the Philippines. The young man was dated to 2022–1792 cal BP, and determined to have suffered from ankylosis in his left hip, causing his femoral head and neck to fuse with the hip socket, likely sometime in his adult life. In turn, this would have left him partially immobile.

This immobility likely contributed to the poor bone remodeling observed in his left leg, but did not explain why his unaffected leg also showed signs of some bone remodeling problem.

The reanalysis of his remains revealed telltale signs of scurvy, limited to his skull and jaw, where constant muscle movement would have led to hemorrhaging of the blood vessels, leading to the scurvy lesions.

Meanwhile, limited mobility meant these same lesions were not observed in the rest of his skeletal remains. However, the scurvy diagnosis does help explain the systemic bone remodeling issues previously observed in both of Burial 4's femora, not just the fused left side, as vitamin C deficiency would have affected his entire skeleton.

"Vitamin C is crucial for bone cells to form new bone tissue, so when it is absent, bones can't repair themselves," explained Dr. Vlok. Scurvy, together with his ankylosis, would have required immense care from his local community, who likely prepared soft foods and regularly repositioned his limbs to prevent pressure sores.

The study underscores the importance of considering the relationship between nutritional deficiencies and physical disabilities and paints a more holistic picture of bioarchaeological care models.
