Hibernation is a risky endeavor for many animals, as they can be taken away by the elements or predators. For several months of the year, queen bumblebees enter a dormant state underground called diapause. While this keeps them hidden, it leaves them vulnerable to waterlogged conditions from rainstorms and snowmelt. But that doesn't seem to be a problem.

According to a new study published in the journal Proceedings of the Royal Society B: Biological Sciences, queen bees can survive complete submersion because they can breathe underwater.

The queen is the only member of the colony to survive the winter. Researchers from the Department of Biology at the University of Ottawa already knew from previous observations that they could survive being submerged, but they didn't know how.

Induced diapause

The team took queens from healthy colonies and induced diapause by placing them in a cold, dark refrigerator to mimic winter conditions. Then they put them in airtight chambers and flooded them with water, ensuring the bees were completely submerged. Some bees were kept underwater for a few hours, while others were kept underwater for up to eight days.

Using specialized equipment, the team measured the gases moving in and out of their bodies, before they were flooded, during submersion and for up to a week after they were removed from the chambers. They also tested for lactate, a chemical that accumulates in the body when oxygen levels are low.

The findings revealed that during submersion, the queens continued to produce carbon dioxide at a low but detectable rate, proving they were still respiring underwater.

Before being submerged, the queens produced the normal amount of carbon dioxide expected for their dormant state. But once underwater, there was a massive drop in the CO2 they released. This shift happened quickly and remained steady whether the bees were submerged for a few hours or the full eight days.

However, this underwater breathing wasn't enough to meet all their needs, so they relied on a backup anaerobic energy system, which led to lactate accumulation in their bodies.

Surviving submersion

After being pulled out of the water, the queens showed a dramatic initial spike in carbon dioxide production as their bodies recovered and worked to clear the lactate.

"This study reveals that diapausing bumble bee queens survive and recover from prolonged flooding through a combination of underwater respiration and anaerobic metabolism in a state of profound metabolic depression," wrote the team in their paper.

While the team demonstrated that bees exchange gases, they do not know the exact physical mechanism of how they breathe underwater. That will have to wait for future studies.
