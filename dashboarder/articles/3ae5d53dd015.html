In recent years, investing in US stocks has become almost routine for many Indian investors. With just a few clicks on international investing platforms, you can own shares of global tech giants like Apple, Microsoft, Tesla and Nvidia.

But while buying foreign stocks has become easier, the tax rules around them remain complicated. One common mistake is becoming more frequent.

While many investors correctly disclose their foreign shares in Schedule FA of their income tax return, they often forget to report the dividend income earned from those shares.

At first glance, it may seem like a minor oversight. But if ignored, it can lead to additional tax demands, penalties and even scrutiny from the income tax department. Understanding how foreign dividends are taxed and what to do if you missed reporting them can save you from bigger trouble later.

Why foreign dividends must be reported in India

One key rule of Indian taxation is that resident taxpayers are taxed on their global income. This means income earned anywhere in the world including dividends from foreign companies must be reported in India.

Under the Income-tax Act, the total income of a resident includes all income accruing or arising outside India, irrespective of where it is received, says CA Mohit Gupta, Partner, PNAM & Co LLP.

âConsequently, dividend income earned from foreign companiesâincluding US listed companiesâis fully taxable in India, even if such income has already been subjected to withholding tax in the United States,â he says.

This often confuses investors because tax may already have been deducted abroad.

For instance, dividends paid by US companies to non-resident investors are usually subject to withholding tax in the US, typically around 25% under the IndiaâUS Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA).

However, this does not eliminate the tax liability in India.

If a US company declares a dividend of $10, the broker may deduct $2.5 as US tax and credit $7.5 to the investorâs account. For Indian tax purposes, however, the investor must still report the entire $10 dividend in the income tax return.

Such dividend income is taxed under the head âIncome from Other Sourcesâ and is added to the investorâs total income. The tax rate applied is simply the investorâs applicable income-tax slab rate.

To avoid double taxation, India allows investors to claim Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) for the tax already paid abroad.

If an investor falls in the 30% tax bracket, the Indian tax on a $10 dividend would be $3. Since $2.5 has already been deducted in the US, the investor would only need to pay the remaining $0.5 in India after claiming the credit, explains Gupta.

The mistake many investors make in their ITR while disclosing foreign investments and income

The confusion arises from how foreign investments are reported in the income tax return.

Indian taxpayers holding overseas assets must disclose them in Schedule FA (Foreign Assets). This schedule captures the existence and value of foreign investments such as shares, bank accounts or property.

However, Schedule FA only reports the asset and not the income earned from it.

Under Schedule FA, investors must disclose details such as the country where the asset is located, the name of the company or broker, the nature of the asset, the date of acquisition, the initial investment value, the highest value during the year and the closing value, explains Swapnil Aggarwal, Director at VSRK Capital.

âThough Schedule FA is primarily concerned with the existence and valuation of the shares, the income earned on the shares, e.g., dividends, would also have to be declared in the income section of the tax return, under the head of other sources, as this would still be taxable in India,â he adds.

This is where many investors slip up. They report their US stock holdings but overlook the small dividend amounts credited during the year.

Can the tax department detect undisclosed foreign income?

Some investors may assume that foreign dividend income is difficult for Indian authorities to track but that may not be true.

India participates in global frameworks that allow automatic exchange of financial information between countries. Through these arrangements, financial institutions and governments share details of accounts and income earned by non-residents.

According to Aggarwal, this includes information related to dividend income and financial accounts held abroad. Once this data reaches Indian authorities, it is matched with the income reported in taxpayersâ returns.

If there is a mismatch, the taxpayer may receive a notice seeking clarification.

Will the Black Money Act penalty of â¹10 lakh apply if you donât disclose foreign income?

The possibility of a â¹10 lakh penalty under the Black Money Act often alarms investors who realise they may have missed reporting foreign income. But in most cases where the foreign shares themselves were disclosed, that fear may be misplaced.

The Black Money (Undisclosed Foreign Income and Assets) Act primarily targets situations where foreign assets themselves are hidden from the tax authorities.

âIf the foreign shares have already been disclosed in Schedule FA but the dividend income was inadvertently not reported, the matter generally falls under under-reporting of income under the Income-tax Act rather than the Black Money Act,â says Gupta.

In such cases, the tax department typically deals with it under Section 270A, which governs penalties for under-reporting or misreporting of income.

The penalty may amount to 50% of the tax payable on the under-reported income, and in more serious cases of misreporting, it can go up to 200% of the tax amount.

What to do if you have not disclosed dividend income from foreign shares?

Fortunately, the tax law provides taxpayers a way to correct such omissions voluntarily.

Under the Updated Return mechanism introduced under Section 139(8A), taxpayers can revise their income tax return within 24 months from the end of the relevant assessment year, subject to payment of additional tax.

If an investor realises that foreign dividend income was not reported, they can file an updated return and disclose the missing income.

However, according to Gupta this comes at a cost of:

â¢ the tax payable on the additional income,

â¢ interest under Sections 234A, 234B, and 234C, where applicable, and

â¢ additional tax, calculated as:

o 25% of the aggregate tax and interest if the updated return is filed within 12 months, or

o 50% of the aggregate tax and interest if filed between 12 and 24 months.

While this increases the tax payable, that voluntary correction significantly reduces the risk of penalties and prolonged litigation.

In case a taxpayer chooses not to correct the omission and the tax authorities subsequently detect the unreported foreign income, the consequences could be more severe, cautions Gupta. These may include:

â¢ reopening of the tax assessment under Sections 147 and 148,

â¢ demand for unpaid tax and interest,

â¢ penalties under Section 270A for under-reporting or misreporting of income (up to 200% of the tax), and

â¢ heightened scrutiny of foreign assets and financial accounts.

Ignoring the issue, on the other hand, could prove far more expensive if the tax authorities later detect the discrepancy.

The paperwork foreign investors should not ignore

For investors holding foreign shares, documentation also plays an important role in staying compliant.

Experts recommend collecting detailed statements from foreign brokers each year.

Investors should keep records such as transaction statements, Form 1099 issued by US brokers, annual stock summaries and dividend payout details, along with information on US taxes deducted, according to Deepashree Shetty, Partner, BDO India.

These documents help substantiate both the income earned and foreign tax paid, which is necessary while filing the return and claiming foreign tax credit.

Global investing has opened new opportunities for Indian investors, but it has also introduced new layers of tax compliance.

For investors who may have missed reporting them in the past, correcting the error early through an updated return is often the simplest and least painful solution.
