Justice A.C. Behera of the High Court of Orissa, on February 19, 2026, ordered the eviction of a son and his wife from his motherâs self-occupied house by holding that a son has no legal right to live in his motherâs property against her wishes. This judgement came in response to a case filed in 2019 by Smt Samal, a mother of four, who alleged that one of her sons and his wife (daughter-in-law) had occupied her house and tortured her, forcing her to shift to a rented house arranged by her other sons and married daughter.

In her submission, she told the court that when she initially complained about this torture in 2012, a village meeting was convened (August 27, 2012), during which her son, who presently resides in the house, assured her that he would not mistreat her. However, he began ill-treating both his mother and father again. Subsequently, a second village meeting was held. During the meeting, the son was ordered to vacate the property; however, he did not comply.

She recounted an incident to the court to convey the extent of threat she felt in her own house. According to her, she and her husband came back to her house on March 9, 2019, after visiting her married daughter. As she returned to her home, her daughter-in-law (her sonâs wife) started scolding her upon seeing her. When she informed her son (who occupies the house) about this incident, he did now allowing her (mother) and her husband (father) to stay in that house; instead, he assaulted and threatened them to go away from and find another place to live.

The next day (on March 10, 2019), she filed an FIR regarding this matter. However, the police did not take any action. She subsequently filed a case in court. The trial court and the first appellate court both ruled in her favour, ordering the eviction of her son and his wife. But the son filed an appeal against these orders in the Orissa High Court.

The son argued in court that her mother had no money to buy this land and build the house, emphasising that they all lived as a joint family, with all the sons working outside the village and contributing financially to the household. The son alleged that she bought this land and constructed the house using family money, asserting that the property was ancestral and not her self-occupied property.

On February 19, 2026, the Orissa High Court ruled in favour of the mother and ordered the sonâs eviction.

According to Sharma, the Court found that the property stood solely in the name of the mother and there was no evidence to establish that it was joint family property or that the son had contributed to its acquisition.

Sharma said: "Consequently, his occupation was held to be permissive in nature and liable to be terminated by the true owner. Once such permission was withdrawn, the son and daughter-in-law could not claim any independent right of residence."

According to Sharma, the Court further relied on precedent to reiterate that ownership confers an absolute right to enjoy and possess the property, including the right to seek eviction of any family member whose occupation is not legally protected.

The Orissa High Court also cited another case (Marabasappa (dead) by Lrs. and others Vrs. Ningappa (dead) by LRs and others, reported in (2011) 9 SCC 451) where it was held that, when the properties stand in the name of the Plaintiffâs mother-in-law, the mother-in-law becomes the full owner of the property by operation of Section 14 of the Hindu Succession Act, 1956.

Thus, the high court rejected the sonâs joint family funds argument.

The Orissa also cited another case (Sachin and another Vrs. Jhabbu Lal and another, reported in AIR 2017 (Delhi) 1) where the court had held that when the house is the self-acquired house of the parents, then, the son, whether married or unmarried, has no legal right to live in that house, and he can live in that house only at the mercy of his parents up to the time the parents allow. Merely because the parents have allowed him to live in the house so long as his relations with the parents were cordial does not mean that the parents have to bear his burden throughout his life.

The Orissa High Court said that, in this aspect, the propositions of law have already been clarified by the courts and the Supreme Court in the ratio of the following decisions:

(i) In the case of Ritesh Khatri vs Shyam Sundar Khatri, reported in 2026 (1) CCC 110 (Raj.) that

If a father allows his son/daughter to continue possessing his house or property or a portion of it, the same does not, by itself, create any legal right in favour of the child to claim that property as his own, unless the property is ancestral or of Hindu undivided property. An injunction against the children, sought for by the father, is allowed.

(ii) In the case of Sachin and another vs Jhabbu Lal and another, reported in AIR 2017 (Delhi) 1 in Para No.15 that

where, the house is the self-acquired house of the parents, then the son, whether married or unmarried, has no legal right to live in that house and he can live in that house only at the mercy of his parents up to the time the parents allow. Merely because the parents have allowed him to live in the house so long as his relations with the parents were cordial does not mean that the parents have to bear his burden throughout his life.
