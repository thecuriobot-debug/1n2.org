A century of change: the legacy of International Women’s Day

Marking 115 years, International Women’s Day traces its roots from early rights movements to today’s global push for women’s equality and recognition.

Every year on March 8, people around the world celebrate International Women’s Day, a global day recognizing women’s achievements and promoting gender equality. International Women’s Day celebrates the social, economic, cultural and political achievements of women, while also calling for action towards gender equality.

This year marks a major milestone for International Women’s Day. First recognized in 1911, the day marks 115 years of advocacy and ongoing efforts towards more opportunities and rights for women worldwide.

What is International Women’s Day?

International Women’s Day, observed annually on March 8, is a globally recognized day dedicated to advocating for and advancing women’s rights. They also aim to recognize the achievements of women in a multitude of areas, including science, politics, education, sports and the arts.

Celebrated by millions of people around the world, the day highlights the social, economic, cultural and political achievements of women throughout history, and the ones they continue to make today.

Beyond celebrating these accomplishments, the observance encourages people, communities and organizations to address continuing challenges, including unequal pay, educational barriers and limited representation in leadership roles throughout different industries.

The history behind the day

International Women’s Day has been observed for more than half a century, originating in 1911 after the growing labor and women’s rights movements. The day is symbolized by three colors: green, purple and white. These colors represent justice, dignity, hope and solidarity, originating from the Women’s Social and Political Union in the United Kingdom.

The day became officially recognized by the United Nations in 1975 and is now celebrated worldwide, with some countries even recognizing it as an official holiday.

Over time, it has grown into a major global movement. Communities and organizations mark the day with events, educational programs and discussions aimed at raising awareness on issues women face.

Understanding the timeline

The early foundations of International Women’s Day started in the 1700’s, with the writing of “Remember the Ladies,” a letter written by Abigail Adams urging lawmakers to consider women’s rights.

Moving forward into the labor and suffrage movements, we see women textile workers protesting conditions in New York City in 1857. In 1903, Emmeline Pankhurst founded the Women’s Social and Political Union in the United Kingdom to campaign for women’s suffrage. In 1909, the first National Women’s Day was organized in the United States.

In 1911, the first International Women’s Day was celebrated in several European countries, and in the mid-20th century, more countries jumped in to recognize the day. In 1975, the United Nations recognized the day, and in 1977, it invited member states to officially observe a day for women’s rights.

Now in 2026, International Women’s Day is marking 115 years since the first celebration, and continues to grow as a global movement advocating for gender equality.

Milestones in women’s rights

As we comb through history in light of International Women’s Day, we can recognize the achievements of women of the past. Going back to 1848, we saw the Seneca Falls Convention, which was the first women’s rights convention in the United States. It was organized by two women, Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott, and launched the formal women’s rights movement.

In 1849, Elizabeth Blackwell became the first woman in the United States to earn a medical degree. In 1851, Sojourner Truth delivered her famous “Ain’t I a Woman?” speech, advocating for both racial and gender equality.

Moving to the 1900s, Amelia Earhart became the first woman to fly solo nonstop across the Atlantic Ocean in 1932. In 1955, Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat on a segregated bus, helping spark the Montgomery Bus Boycott and the modern civil rights movement. In 1960, the FDA approved the first birth control pill, an important milestone in reproductive autonomy for women.

Moving forward to today, Kamala Harris became not only the first woman, but also the first woman of color to serve as Vice President of the United States. These milestones highlight major breakthroughs in the women’s rights movement and breakthroughs across industries that expanded opportunities and rights for women.

Observing International Women’s Day

As International Women’s Day approaches this year, the main goal is to celebrate women’s achievements, raise awareness about gender inequality and encourage action to promote equal opportunities for women worldwide.
