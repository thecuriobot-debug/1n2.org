![FAF synthesis of SiC NW from waste glass. Credit: Matter (2026). DOI: 10.1016/j.matt.2026.102664 Fluorine-assisted flash process upcycles waste glass to high quality silicon carbide nanowires in seconds](https://scx1.b-cdn.net/csz/news/800a/2026/fluorine-assisted-flas-1.jpg)

Engineering silicon carbide (SiC) with tailored morphologies for electronics and structural reinforcement materials has always been a costly and time-consuming affair, but scientists can now do it in a flash. A new study shows how discarded glass and silicon-rich coal waste can be turned into valuable SiC nanowires in seconds using a process known as Fluorine-Assisted Flash (FAF) Joule heating, where a quick pulse of electricity instantly heats up the reaction mixture to extremely high temperatures.

In FAF, the fluorine additives trigger the catalytic materials, such as the iron oxides found naturally in waste glass, to act as seeds that drive selective growth of one-dimensional nanowires in under a minute and with an impressive yield of 96%. When used as a reinforcement material in composites, SiC nanowires emerged as clear winners over SiC powders in providing hardness and wear resistance. The findings are published in Matter.

A sustainable way to nanowires

SiC is a ceramic material built from strong covalent bonds between silicon and carbon, making it one of the hardest materials known, surpassed by only a handful of substances such as diamond.

Beyond its exceptional mechanical strength, SiC is also a wide band-gap material widely used in the semiconductor industry, capable of operating under extreme temperatures and high voltages.

Nanowires of SiC have all the properties of bulk SiC, and more. Their 1D structure with a high aspect ratio, meaning they are much longer than they are wide, makes them better thermal conductors and resist wear, making them a lucrative material for industries.

However, synthesizing high-quality, morphology-controlled SiC nanowires using current methods still calls for expensive starting materials, extremely high temperatures, and costly catalysts, making these processes economically and environmentally expensive.

The researchers addressed these issues by using FAF Joule heating to synthesize SiC nanowires. In this process, silicon sources such as waste glass or coal ash were first ground into powder and mixed with a carbon source like coke or carbon black. A small amount of fluoride, about 1% in the form of sodium fluoride, was then added to the mixture.

This mixture was placed in a small tube and subjected to a high-power electrical pulse, which rapidly increased the temperature to around 2,000 °C within a fraction of a minute. After the intense heating, the material was rapidly cooled, allowing the SiC nanowires to crystallize into a cubic lattice while retaining high crystallinity. The process produced a high nanowire selectivity of up to 78% and a silicon yield of about 96%.

![Morphology-dependent reinforced performance of SiC. Credit: Matter (2026). DOI: 10.1016/j.matt.2026.102664 Fluorine-assisted flash process upcycles waste glass to high quality silicon carbide nanowires in seconds](https://scx1.b-cdn.net/csz/news/800a/2026/fluorine-assisted-flas-2.jpg)

By adjusting the duration of the electrical flash and the amount of fluorine added, the researchers were able to control the length of the nanowires.

Once the SiC nanowires were extracted and cleaned, the team incorporated them into a vinyl ester (VE) resin to create composite materials, in which the nanowires served as reinforcing fillers. These composites were then subjected to a series of mechanical and thermal tests to evaluate how the nanowires affected the material's performance and compared it to SiC powders available on the market.

The new SiC nanowires outperformed the powders. Even at 1 wt% loading, the nanowires boosted the Young's modulus by 41%, increased hardness by 62%, and enhanced wear resistance by 28%.

The benefits of this new technique don't end there. Due to the FAF Joule heating process being so incredibly fast, it reduced the water and energy consumption and the carbon dioxide emissions of the nanowires synthesis process by about 90%.

The study establishes FAF Joule heating as a fast and sustainable approach to the synthesis of carbide nanowires. The researchers highlight that the new strategy can be scaled up and applied to other advanced carbide nanomaterials, including those of titanium, niobium, and boron.
