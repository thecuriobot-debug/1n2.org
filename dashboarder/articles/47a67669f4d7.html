Oil prices are leaving airlines with nowhere to hide

This year’s disruption — less than two weeks into the US and Israeli airstrikes on Iran — has the potential to be even worse if oil prices remain near $100 a barrel for an extended period. Jet fuel, the second-largest expense for airlines behind labor, already jumped to as much as $4.11 a gallon in New York on March 5 from a low of $1.92 last May.

Among the different modes of transportation, airlines are the most exposed to oil price increases because they are difficult to pass through to customers. Maritime shipping, railroads and trucking companies all have fuel surcharges that automatically add the extra energy costs to customers’ cargo bills. There’s a bit of lag for the surcharges to kick in — a week or two for truck companies and a month or two for railroads — so there’s a temporary squeeze on margins. Freight customers — retailers, manufacturers, builders and others — can absorb the increased cost for a while because cargo isn’t one of their top expenses. It helps that inflation-adjusted freight rates are much lower than they were five years ago.

The airlines have nowhere to hide from an oil-price spike. They sell directly to the consumer. Carriers either eat the expense or pass it along, knowing that higher airfares dampen demand. That demand destruction could mean that price increases don’t turn into higher revenue, said Bijan Vasigh, a professor at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University. There’s also a delay for reaping fare increases because most passengers buy tickets two weeks or more in advance.

US airlines have mostly abandoned hedges on oil and jet fuel because the constant expense outweighed the rare gains. The oil-price volatility over the last five years had driven up hedging costs to almost $150 million a year for Southwest Airlines Co., Chief Executive Officer Bob Jordan said during a Morgan Stanley conference in September. Alaska Air Group Inc., one of the last holdouts on hedging, had concluded all its fuel hedges by the end of last year.

Despite the high fuel prices, most airline profits recovered in 2022 from steep losses during the pandemic. It’s hard to overstate the crisis that Covid-19 caused airlines, shutting off passengers suddenly in March 2020 like closing a spigot. Debt piled up and the federal government intervened with support. Weaker airlines, mostly low-cost ones, have struggled since to turn a profit.

There also was a brutal four-year period beginning in 2011 when WTI crude prices averaged more than $95 a barrel and squeezed airline earnings. The terrorist attacks on Sept. 11, 2001, also caused a crisis. The industry reacted with airline mergers that has left only four main airlines and a few struggling low-cost carriers. Fewer competitors exist to keep airfares in check, and many airports are dominated by one carrier, such as United in Atlanta and American in Dallas.

If fuel prices remain high for the rest of this year, the burden could sink Spirit Airlines, which reached an agreement with creditors in February to exit bankruptcy. Other vulnerable carriers are Frontier Group Holdings Inc. and JetBlue Airways Corp., which both posted net losses last year. Among the four large US airlines, American has the weakest finances, ending last year with total debt of $36.5 billion and posting negative free cash flow. It’s no wonder that American has led the share decline among US airlines, dropping 28% this year while Frontier has declined 23%.

![814x-1 (28) 814x-1 (28)](https://img.etimg.com/photo/msid-42031747/et-logo.jpg)

A short-lived spike in crude oil prices would still hurt first-quarter airline earnings but wouldn’t sink the possibility of healthy profits the rest of the year. A prolonged increase would drive up airfares and might even thin the herd as weaker airlines succumb to the financial burden. That would mean even more airline concentration and structurally higher airfares.
