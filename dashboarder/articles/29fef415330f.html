The first patient I met as a medical student was a middle-aged delivery man and a former track athlete. He was recovering from his second amputation due to uncontrolled type 2 diabetes. When he told me he never realized how much his “high sugars” could cost him, I wasn’t sure what to say.

I rotated at four busy New York City hospitals during my medical education. Each day, I saw patients with similar stories — people living with the consequences of poorly controlled chronic disease. I learned to assess for strokes, manage heart failure exacerbations, and explain the vascular impact of diabetes in fancy physiologic terms. I learned to treat the complications of chronic disease well, but I learned little of how to prevent them.

I am now a fourth-year medical student pursuing a career in internal medicine with a focus on chronic disease prevention. I entered medical school eager to learn how I could shape my patients’ health through nutrition, lifestyle, and preventive medicine.

But these topics have largely been absent from my training. Now, as I’m poised to graduate, I feel unprepared to address the chronic diseases that will affect the patients I will soon care for.

Over the past two years, Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and surgeon general nominee Casey Means, among others, have argued that the medical establishment pays too little attention to nutrition and lifestyle in physician training. Their critiques resonate with my experience in medical school: learn to react first, focus on prevention later.

Modern medicine is extraordinarily good at intervening once the body begins to fail. Training and hospitals alike are organized around specialties: cardiology for heart attacks, nephrology for kidney failure, neurology for strokes, vascular surgery for ischemic limbs. This siloed structure reinforces a reactive model of health care. By the time patients reach a specialist, their disease has manifested organ-specific consequences, and we treat each piece separately. We leave the role of prevention to primary care physicians who are already overburdened with patients chronically sicker than ever before.

These conditions share the same upstream drivers: metabolic dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and an obesogenic environment that makes healthy choices difficult. We future physicians are taught to think of chronic diseases as separate entities rather than the result of these shared root causes. For instance, during a three-week outpatient internal medicine rotation, my classmates and I did not receive any formal training on nutrition or lifestyle counseling, despite the fact that primary care visits are a key touchpoint for discussing behavioral changes with patients.

These are missed opportunities to shape trainees into physicians who value and believe in preventive medicine. We cannot solve our chronic disease epidemic, which affects more than 90% of American adults and accounts for 90% of our health care spending, with a health care training model designed for acute crises.

A proactive health care system means training the next generation of doctors to think about how to alter the course of chronic diseases before they present as acute problems, namely through prevention and lifestyle-based practices.

However, this is not the reality of medical training today. Preventive and lifestyle-based approaches continue to receive little emphasis in medical education.

For example, most medical schools fail to meet even the minimum 25 hours of nutrition instruction recommended by the National Academy of Sciences in 1985. Much of what institutions count toward this requirement are lectures embedded in basic science and not clinically applicable. Because prevention and lifestyle medicine are largely absent from national medical board examinations, medical educators often cannot justify dedicating limited curricular time to topics not formally tested upon.

In turn, studies consistently show that physicians do not feel prepared to provide effective nutrition and lifestyle counseling.

During my clinical year, I observed acute care medicine at its finest. Mentally, I noted how many seemingly acute problems were actually end-organ manifestations of inadequately treated risk factors like insulin resistance, chronic inflammation, and obesity. During rounds, we would adjust medications and consult specialty teams for procedures, but seldom did we discuss the connection between the chronic diseases we saw daily, initiate lifestyle counseling, or provide resources for behavior change.

As a third-year medical student, I tried taking some of this education into my own hands. I spent several months designing a lecture on chronic disease prevention, highlighting what I wish I had learned: the commonality between chronic diseases, the causal role of our obesogenic environment, and simple tools for improving health through nutrition and lifestyle. I delivered this lecture to more than 100 second-year medical students at my school. Many students agreed that nutrition and lifestyle education are lacking in medical training. Others were more concerned about the credibility of a student-led lecture on nutrition, which can be both socially complicated and controversial in practicality. While a subsequent lecture was initially planned for the upcoming school year, my institution discontinued it since nutrition contact hours were already being met and curricular time was limited.

Evidence-based education on prevention and lifestyle medicine must be interspersed longitudinally and consistently across each stage of medical school with the help of allied professionals including registered dieticians. This approach would highlight the role of lifestyle medicine and prevention within each organ system and provide trainees competency-based nutrition and lifestyle knowledge and skillsets that extend beyond the classroom to patient care. For implementation to be sustainable, the value of these topics must be reflected in board examinations that shape what is taught.

Medical education reflects the larger health care system it prepares trainees to enter. For decades, acute illness defined American medicine, so physician training was structured around interventions that address acute problems. Today, chronic diseases dominate. The majority of conditions physicians encounter in daily practice, regardless of their medical specialty, are influenced by the same short list of lifestyle factors.

I’m glad to see that 53 medical schools recently agreed to add more nutrition education to the curriculum in response to Kennedy’s request. But that should be the start of a revolution in medical education, not the end of it. If chronic diseases now define modern medicine, why do we continue to treat prevention as supplemental rather than central in physician training?

Lauren Rice is a fourth-year medical student in New York City pursuing a career in internal medicine with interests in chronic disease prevention and medical education.
