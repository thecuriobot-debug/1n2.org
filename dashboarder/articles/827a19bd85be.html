Synopsis

Kharg Island, Iran's primary crude oil export terminal, has become a critical strategic asset in the ongoing West Asia conflict. Despite escalating military actions targeting Iran, the island remains untouched, a decision analysts believe could significantly influence the war's direction and global oil markets.

Iran’s oil lifeline in the GulfKharg Island lies about 25 km off Iran’s coast and has served as the country’s main crude export terminal since the 1960s, when it was developed with the help of US oil company Amoco. The facility can load up to 7 million barrels of oil per day, according to a report by the Financial Times, and handles roughly 90% of Iran’s crude exports.

Subsea pipelines connect the island to major oilfields in southern Iran. Long jetties stretch into deep water to allow supertankers to load crude, while large storage tanks and worker housing cover much of the island’s southern area.

The terminal’s importance to the Iranian economy is widely acknowledged. Richard Nephew, a former US deputy special envoy for Iran, told the Financial Times: “The economy bottoms out without it.”

Why the island has not been attacked

The loading activity took place during Operation Epic Fury, the military campaign launched by the US and Israel. It remains unclear whether operations have continued as the conflict intensified because no satellite images of Kharg Island have been released since that day.

Washington has long treated the island as a red line because of the risks involved. Destroying or seizing Kharg could severely damage Iran’s economy but might also trigger retaliation against energy infrastructure across the Gulf.

Trump’s strategic dilemma

President Donald Trump has indicated that the US campaign could expand beyond military and nuclear targets, raising questions about whether energy infrastructure could eventually be included.

Some officials and analysts have discussed the possibility of seizing Kharg Island to restrict Iran’s oil revenues. Control of the terminal could dramatically reduce Tehran’s income from crude exports, which remain a key source of government funding.

White House energy adviser Jarrod Agen said in an interview with Fox Business that the long-term goal could be to remove Iranian oil resources from what he called “terrorists”.

However, other policy experts warn that striking the island could weaken any future Iranian government if political change occurs after the war.

A target with global consequences

Kharg Island has been a strategic vulnerability for decades. During the Iran-Iraq war in the 1980s, the island was repeatedly bombed because of its role in the country’s oil exports.

Today, its importance is even greater. Nearly all of Iran’s crude exports pass through the island before tankers travel through the Strait of Hormuz toward Asian markets, particularly China.

Because of this concentration of infrastructure, any disruption at Kharg could quickly affect global energy markets and oil prices.

The island at the centre of the war’s economic stakes

For now, Kharg Island remains operational as the conflict continues. Tankers still load crude there and shipping routes remain under close monitoring.

But as military pressure on Iran increases, the island’s future may depend on decisions taken in Washington. Whether the US chooses to leave Kharg untouched, strike it, or attempt to seize it could shape both the trajectory of the war and the stability of global oil supplies.
