The government has sought to reassure citizens that there are no supply constraints, but the situation on the ground — marked by anxiety and growing panic among people — appears to tell a very different story. Many small businesses, such as tea stalls and roadside eateries across the country, depend heavily on LPG for cooking but are now facing rising prices and limited availability.

Vijay Pal, a poha seller in Noida's Film City, has increased the price of his poha by more than 30 per cent as the LPG cylinder he usually bought from the black market had already shot up. When asked if LPG cylinders are not available in the market, he told Economic Times, "LPG cylinders are available but at a higher price. The cylinders, which were once available at Rs 100 are now being sold at Rs 300."

Samosa seller Pramod Kumar has also increased the price of samosas by 50 per cent. He fears whether he would be able to open his shop the next day. When Pramod tried to place the next day's cylinder order with his supplier, the response was uncertain. "I've spoken to someone who might bring one from Ghaziabad. If it doesn't come, then we won't open the shop," he said. "Nearby, around Sector 18's Atta Market, several dhabas and food joints have already begun shutting down," he told India Today.

LPG cylinder crisis in India

Amid growing concerns over LPG availability, small businesses and street vendors across Noida, Greater Noida and Ghaziabad say the situation is becoming increasingly difficult, with commercial cylinders either unavailable or being sold at sharply inflated prices. Amit Upadhyay, who runs a packaging unit in Ecotech-3 in Greater Noida, told Times of India his factory was forced to halt operations on Tuesday after commercial LPG cylinders could not be procured through official supply channels. “Normally, a cylinder costs around ₹1,900, but in the black market it is being sold for up to ₹3,000. Even after paying that much, there is no guarantee it will be delivered the same day,” he said. On regular days, the unofficial market price usually ranges between ₹2,000 and ₹2,200.

Several vendors said suppliers are now demanding advance payments because of limited stock. A street food vendor operating near Raj Nagar District Centre in Ghaziabad said distributors are asking customers to pay first to secure a cylinder. “They say supply is short and we must pay in advance to reserve it,” he said.

For many small food cart operators, the sudden rise in LPG prices is hitting already fragile profit margins. A paratha seller in Sector 16A said he cannot increase prices to offset the cost of fuel. “I sell two parathas for ₹20. If LPG becomes this expensive, I cannot sell them for ₹40. No one will buy. If this continues, I may have to shut the stall and return to my village in Banda,” he said.

LPG crisis: Vendors face the heat

A large number of roadside vendors in the twin cities operate without formal commercial LPG connections, relying instead on informal supply chains that become highly unstable whenever official distribution is disrupted.For vendors who depend on daily earnings, the situation has become even more challenging. Instead of purchasing full cylinders, many buy small quantities of LPG refilled from illegal bottling units to keep their carts running for a day.

A tea stall operator in Indirapuram said the cost of these daily refills has more than doubled. “Earlier, we could refill just enough gas for a day at around ₹85 per kg. Now they are asking nearly ₹200 per kg. If we buy five or six kilograms for the day, the cost becomes too high,” he said.

Such daily refills are common among vendors selling tea, momos, chowmein and snacks, where earnings barely cover day-to-day expenses.

Much of the LPG circulating in the black market is believed to be diverted from subsidised domestic cylinders meant for households. Under the Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana, a 14.2-kg domestic cylinder receives a subsidy of ₹300 and currently costs around ₹613, while the non-subsidised domestic cylinder is priced at about ₹913.
